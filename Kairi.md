# KAIRI: A Formal Term for Quantized Relative Difference

## Overview

This document introduces **KAIRI** as a formal mathematical and conceptual term, defined in the context of a post-binary, multi-base representational framework. Drawing inspiration from the user's directive to transcend binary (base 2) limitations and base-10 artifacts, KAIRI emphasizes relational differences across higher bases (e.g., base 11 and 13 for "play" or exploration) and foundational primes (base 2 and 3 for language construction). 

Frame independence—previously a conceptual crutch for absolute positioning—is discarded in favor of pure relationality: everything is defined by *difference*, and how that difference relates to itself (self-similarity) and to the broader relational web (everything else). KAIRI quantizes these differences, providing a discrete, measurable structure for relational dynamics.

This term is proposed for use in theoretical mathematics, computational linguistics, and relational ontology, with etymological ties to Japanese and Pacific Islander cultural meanings to ground it in oceanic, communal, and resilient metaphors.

## Formal Definition

**KAIRI**: A *relative difference quantized*.

### Precise Formulation
Let \( D \) represent a relational space where entities are defined solely by their differences: \( D = \{ (e_i, e_j) \mid \delta(e_i, e_j) \}, \) with \( \delta \) as a difference metric (e.g., distance, divergence, or transformation).

A **KAIRI** is a discrete quantization of \( \delta \), denoted \( K(e_i, e_j) = q(\delta(e_i, e_j)) \), where:
- \( q: \mathbb{R}^+ \to \mathcal{Q} \) is a quantization function mapping continuous differences to a finite, discrete set \( \mathcal{Q} \) (e.g., integers in a chosen base).
- **Relativity**: \( K \) is frame-invariant; it depends only on the pair \( (e_i, e_j) \), not absolute positions. Self-relation \( K(e_i, e_i) = 0 \) (identity), and reflexivity holds: \( K(e_j, e_i) = K(e_i, e_j) \) or signed variants for directionality.
- **Multi-Base Encoding**: Quantization occurs in non-standard bases to avoid binary artifacts:
  - Base 11/13 for exploratory "play" (higher cardinality for richer relations).
  - Base 2/3 for foundational "language make" (prime sparsity for minimal syntax).
- **Relational Closure**: A KAIRI network forms a graph \( G = (E, K) \), where edges are KAIRIs, ensuring transitivity approximations: \( K(e_i, e_k) \approx f(K(e_i, e_j), K(e_j, e_k)) \) for some aggregation \( f \) (e.g., min, sum, or fractal self-similarity).

**Example**: In a base-11 system, the difference between entities A (encoded 1A) and B (encoded 2B) yields \( \delta = 1.5 \) leagues (nautical nod). Quantized: \( K(A,B) = 2_{11} \) (base-11 "2"), representing a discrete "village-step" across the relational sea.

This definition rejects frame crutches: no global coordinates; only differences, self-related and web-entangled.
